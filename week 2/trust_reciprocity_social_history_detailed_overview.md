# Detailed Overview: *Trust, Reciprocity, and Social History* (Berg, Dickhaut, & McCabe, 1995)

## Citation
Berg, J., Dickhaut, J., & McCabe, K. (1995). *Trust, reciprocity, and social history*. Games and Economic Behavior, 10, 122–142.

## High-Level Purpose of the Chapter/Paper
This paper designs and tests an experimental version of a **trust game** to examine whether people behave according to the standard economic prediction of strict self-interest, or whether they instead display **trust** and **reciprocity**. The authors are especially interested in whether cooperative behavior can be explained by:

1. repeated-game incentives,
2. reputations,
3. contractual commitments,
4. punishment threats,
5. social history and norms,
6. or intrinsic tendencies toward trust and reciprocity.

Their key claim is that even in a **one-shot, double-blind interaction**, where strategic reputation-building and punishment are ruled out, many participants still send money and many counterparts return money. This provides evidence that behavior is not fully captured by the subgame-perfect prediction of pure self-interest.

---

## Central Research Question
The paper asks whether individuals will:

- **trust** an anonymous stranger by sending money in a one-shot game, and
- **reciprocate** by returning money after having the opportunity to exploit the other player.

It also asks whether prior information about others’ past behavior—called **social history**—changes the level of trust and reciprocity.

---

## Core Economic Setup
The study uses a two-person sequential exchange game:

- A subject in **Room A** receives **$10**.
- The Room A subject can send any amount \(M_A\in\{0,1,2,\dots,10\}\) to an anonymous counterpart in **Room B**.
- Any amount sent is **tripled** by the experimenter, so the Room B subject receives **3M_A**.
- The Room B subject then decides how much to return, denoted \(M_B\), from the tripled amount.

### Payoff functions
- Room A payoff: \(P_A = 10 - M_A + M_B\)
- Room B payoff: \(P_B = 3M_A - M_B\)

Under strict self-interest and backward induction:

- Room B should return **0** for any positive amount received.
- Anticipating this, Room A should send **0**.

So the **subgame-perfect equilibrium** is:

- \(M_A = 0\)
- \(M_B = 0\)

This is the benchmark prediction the experiment tests.

## Major Subtopics and Detailed Content

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Standard economic assumption under scrutiny
The introduction begins with the classic economic premise that people act in their own self-interest. Combined with assumptions like unrestricted consumption and perfect information, that premise implies that behavior deviating from self-interest is irrational.

The authors argue that many important real-world transactions depend on **trust**, including:

- labor exchange,
- credit relationships,
- contractual performance,
- and other settings where one party must act before guarantees are available.

### 1.2 Why trust matters theoretically
The paper positions trust as a challenge to narrow rational-choice explanations. If people still cooperate when repeated interaction, punishment, and reputation are absent, then some other mechanism must be operating.

### 1.3 Evolutionary and social explanations
The authors discuss alternative views, including:

- **evolutionary arguments** that trust and reciprocity may be adaptive,
- **social norms** that shape expectations and behavior,
- and the possibility that reciprocity is a fundamental feature of social exchange.

They note that the experiment is intentionally structured to strip away repeated-game incentives and reputational effects, allowing cleaner inference about trust and reciprocity themselves.

---

## 2. Hypotheses and Procedures

This section formalizes the experimental design and connects it to competing behavioral predictions.

### 2.1 Game-theoretic prediction
If subjects reason according to standard backward induction:

- Room B returns nothing.
- Room A therefore sends nothing.

This outcome is the null benchmark.

### 2.2 Trust as costly initiation of exchange
The paper interprets sending money from Room A as an act of **trust** because it is risky:

- the sender gives up certain money,
- the receiver can exploit the sender,
- and there is no formal enforcement.

### 2.3 Reciprocity as a positive response
Returning money from Room B is treated as **reciprocity** because the receiver had the opportunity to keep everything yet chooses to reward the sender’s trust.

### 2.4 Alternative hypotheses
The authors derive alternatives to the self-interest benchmark, including:

- some Room A subjects will send positive amounts,
- some Room B subjects will return positive amounts,
- and the amount sent may be positively associated with the amount returned.

They explicitly discuss the possibility of a **positive correlation** between trust and reciprocity: higher amounts sent may elicit greater return behavior.

---

## 3. The Double-Blind Procedure

One of the most important subtopics in the paper is the experimental control structure.

### 3.1 Why double-blindness matters
The authors emphasize that many earlier experimental results could potentially be influenced by:

- experimenter demand effects,
- social approval concerns,
- fear of judgment,
- or implicit moral pressure.

To reduce those concerns, the trust game is implemented in a **double-blind** format. This means that:

- subjects do not know each other’s identities,
- the experimenter cannot match specific decisions to specific individuals,
- and payment handling is structured to preserve anonymity.

### 3.2 Physical implementation
The figure on page 7 diagrams the setup. Key features include:

- two separate rooms (A and B),
- partitions,
- mailboxes,
- envelopes,
- keys,
- monitors/recorders,
- and a process for moving decisions and money without exposing identities.

Subjects put decisions and money into envelopes with coded but identity-shielding procedures. The design is meant to make the interaction as anonymous as possible while still allowing the sequential game to be carried out.

### 3.3 Why this is methodologically important
This procedure is central to the paper’s contribution. If trust and reciprocity appear **even here**, then those behaviors cannot easily be dismissed as artifacts of surveillance, repeat-play incentives, or face-to-face pressure.

## 4. Subjects and Sessions

### 4.1 Subject pool
Subjects were recruited from undergraduate students at the University of Minnesota.

### 4.2 Prior participation constraint
Participants had previously taken part in at least one unrelated paid laboratory experiment. This likely helped the researchers use subjects who were familiar with lab procedures while keeping this trust game novel.

### 4.3 Session organization
According to the table of sessions:

- **No-history treatment** involved **32 pairs** over **3 days**.
- **Social-history treatment** involved **28 additional pairs** over later sessions.
- Experiments lasted roughly **60 to 90 minutes**.

---

## 5. No-History Results

This section reports what happened when subjects were given **no information** about previous players’ behavior.

### 5.1 Aggregate finding
The no-history treatment strongly departs from the self-interest equilibrium.

According to the figure and accompanying discussion:

- many Room A subjects sent positive amounts,
- many Room B subjects returned positive amounts,
- and average behavior was well above the zero-zero benchmark.

The figure on page 9 reports approximately:

- **Average amount sent**: **$5.16**
- **Average total return to Room A**: **$4.66**
- **Average payback**: about **0.30**

This means subjects in Room A were willing, on average, to risk over half of their endowment even without any enforcement or history.

### 5.2 Room A decisions under no history
Key observations reported in the text:

- Only **2 of 32** subjects sent **zero**.
- The amounts sent were significantly higher than zero and not consistent with the null hypothesis of no trust.
- High amounts such as **$5** and **$10** occurred often enough to reject the idea that subjects were merely randomizing uniformly across amounts.

Interpretation:

- There is clear evidence of **trust**.
- Subjects did not behave as backward induction predicts.

### 5.3 Room B decisions under no history
The paper also reports strong evidence of reciprocity:

- Of the **28** Room B subjects who received more than **$1**, **30** returned a positive amount?  
  The more precise statement in the paper is that among those receiving meaningful positive transfers, many returned positive amounts, and **11** of the same 28 returned **more than their counterpart sent**.
- This is inconsistent with pure self-interest, since a selfish Room B player should always return zero.

The authors also compute a **Spearman rank correlation** between amount sent and percentage returned. The reported coefficient is approximately **0.51**, indicating a positive association between trust and reciprocal return.

### 5.4 Substantive interpretation
These results show that in a one-shot anonymous interaction:

- people are willing to initiate trust,
- recipients often reciprocate,
- and larger acts of trust tend to be associated with stronger reciprocal responses.

## 6. Social History Results

This section introduces one of the paper’s most important experimental variations.

### 6.1 What “social history” means here
In the social-history treatment, subjects received a report summarizing prior decisions made by others in earlier sessions. This did **not** create repeated interaction with the same partner. Instead, it provided a norm-like informational environment:

- what previous Room A subjects tended to send,
- what previous Room B subjects tended to return.

The goal was to test whether trust and reciprocity become stronger when participants know that others before them have behaved cooperatively.

### 6.2 Theoretical motivation
The authors relate this treatment to theories of **social norms**. If norms influence conduct, then seeing evidence of prior trust and reciprocity should increase willingness to act similarly.

### 6.3 Aggregate pattern under social history
The figure and text show an increase in central tendencies:

- **Average amount sent** rose to about **$5.36**.
- **Average payback** rose to about **$6.46** in total room-A return?  
  More precisely, the figure reports average amounts for sent, total return, and payback, and the text states that payback behavior increased substantially.
- In Room B, **investments of $10** produced an average payback of about **$7.14**, whereas **investments of $10 under no history** produced an average payback around **$3.19**.

### 6.4 Distributional change
The boxplots on page 13 show that **dispersion increased** under social history. The authors note that social history widened the interquartile range, especially for sending behavior. This means the history treatment did not simply shift everyone upward uniformly; it also broadened the range of responses.

### 6.5 Room A decisions with social history
The paper reports:

- Only **3 of 28** Room A subjects sent zero.
- There were increases in the frequency of **$5** and **$10** transfers, though the increase in mean sending alone was not statistically strong enough to support a simple shift-in-level story.

Interpretation:

- Social history did not dramatically change the average amount sent,
- but it appears to have changed the **distribution** and perhaps the confidence with which subjects trusted others.

### 6.6 Room B decisions with social history
This is where the stronger treatment effect appears.

The paper reports:

- Of the **24** Room B subjects who received more than **$1**, **24** returned a positive amount?  
  The text indicates widespread positive return behavior and emphasizes stronger reciprocal response under social history.
- The average payback conditional on large investments rose sharply.
- The **Spearman rank correlation** between amount sent and return percentage increased from roughly **0.51** (no history) to roughly **0.79** (social history).
- Using a permutation/randomization-based comparison, the authors conclude that this increase in correlation is statistically significant.

Interpretation:

- Social history strengthened the mapping from trust to reciprocity.
- Norm information appears to make reciprocation more responsive to the sender’s act.

## 7. Payback Decisions and Joint Earnings

This section examines how the interaction affects final earnings of both players.

### 7.1 Figure on joint earnings
The chart on page 15 plots Room A earnings against Room B earnings and marks regions corresponding to:

- no trust,
- one-third payback,
- break-even for Room A,
- equal total earnings,
- and positive net returns to Room A.

### 7.2 Why this matters
This figure shows that reciprocity was often substantial enough not merely to symbolize goodwill, but to generate materially different payoff distributions.

Some pairs ended in outcomes where:

- Room A earned at least as much as by keeping the initial $10,
- both parties benefited from exchange,
- or returns split the gains more evenly.

### 7.3 Empirical takeaway
The experiment was not just about whether people send or return *something*. It also demonstrates that reciprocity can be large enough to make trust economically meaningful.

---

## 8. Conclusion and Interpretation

The conclusion synthesizes the paper’s main contributions.

### 8.1 Main empirical conclusions
The authors conclude that:

1. **Trust exists** in this one-shot anonymous setting.
2. **Reciprocity exists** in this one-shot anonymous setting.
3. Standard subgame-perfect predictions do **not** describe observed behavior well.
4. **Social history matters**, especially by strengthening reciprocity.

### 8.2 Why this is theoretically important
The results challenge the narrow view that people always maximize immediate private payoff when institutions do not enforce cooperation.

Instead, the findings suggest:

- trust may be a stable feature of human exchange,
- reciprocity may be intrinsic or norm-driven,
- and economic institutions may rely partly on social motivations, not only formal contracts.

### 8.3 Relation to broader economics and social science
The paper implies that markets and organizations may function in part because people bring social norms and reciprocal expectations into exchange. This has implications for:

- contract theory,
- organizational design,
- labor markets,
- development economics,
- and behavioral economics.

## Key Concepts and Terms

### Trust
A costly action taken under vulnerability, where the other party can exploit the actor.

### Reciprocity
A return action that rewards trust rather than taking maximum advantage of the opportunity.

### Subgame-perfect equilibrium
The backward-induction solution concept predicting no sending and no return in this game.

### Double-blind design
A method that prevents both subjects and experimenters from linking choices to specific individuals, reducing demand effects and moral signaling.

### Social history
Information about prior participants’ decisions, intended to simulate a norm environment without introducing direct repeated interaction.

### Spearman rank correlation
A nonparametric measure used to test whether higher sent amounts are associated with higher return behavior.

---

## Most Important Empirical Findings to Remember

1. **The equilibrium prediction failed**: people did not mostly choose \((0,0)\).
2. **Trust was common**: almost all Room A subjects sent something.
3. **Reciprocity was common**: many Room B subjects returned money.
4. **Trust and return were positively related**.
5. **Social history strengthened reciprocity**, especially for larger investments.
6. The **double-blind design** makes the evidence unusually strong because common alternative explanations were minimized.

---

## Why This Chapter/Paper Is Influential
This paper became foundational in behavioral and experimental economics because it gave a clean, memorable demonstration that:

- people often trust strangers even when self-interest predicts they should not,
- people often reciprocate even when exploitation would maximize payoff,
- and social context can shape exchange even when institutions are weak.

It helped establish the trust game as a standard tool for studying:

- pro-social behavior,
- moral motivation,
- institutional trust,
- cooperation,
- and norm formation.

## Concise Study Guide Summary
This paper introduces a one-shot, anonymous investment/trust game to test whether people behave according to pure self-interest. A sender in Room A can send part of a $10 endowment to a receiver in Room B; the sent amount is tripled, and the receiver chooses how much to return. Standard theory predicts no sending and no return. The experiment instead finds substantial positive sending and return behavior, showing evidence of trust and reciprocity. The double-blind design strengthens the validity of this conclusion by reducing reputational and experimenter effects. A social-history treatment, where subjects see summaries of earlier behavior, further increases reciprocity and strengthens the relationship between investment and repayment. The paper argues that economic exchange depends not only on incentives and enforcement but also on norms, trust, and reciprocal behavior.
