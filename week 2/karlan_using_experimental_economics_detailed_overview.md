# Detailed Overview: *Using Experimental Economics to Measure Social Capital and Predict Financial Decisions*  
**Author:** Dean S. Karlan  
**Source:** *American Economic Review* (2005), Vol. 95, No. 5, pp. 1688-1699

## 1. Central Question and Purpose

This paper asks whether experimental economics - especially the **Trust Game** - can measure forms of social capital that matter outside the lab. Rather than treating game behavior as an end in itself, Karlan tests whether actions in experimental games predict **real financial behavior** among poor women participating in a Peruvian microfinance program.

The paper focuses on whether:
- **Player A's action** in the Trust Game actually measures **trust**, as commonly claimed.
- **Player B's action** measures **trustworthiness** or reciprocity.
- These game-based measures predict meaningful outcomes such as:
  - loan default,
  - dropout from the lending program due to default or discipline,
  - voluntary savings behavior.

The study's broader contribution is methodological: it examines whether experimental games can serve as **field measures of economically relevant social capital**, not just laboratory constructs.

---

## 2. Main Thesis

Karlan's core conclusions are:

1. **Player B behavior in the Trust Game is a useful predictor of trustworthiness.**  
   People who return more money in the game are more likely to repay loans and less likely to default later.

2. **Player A behavior is not a clean measure of trust.**  
   The paper argues that passing more money may partly capture **risk-taking or a propensity to gamble**, not just trust.

3. **The Trust Game predicts real-world behavior better than the Public Goods Game in this setting.**  
   The Public Goods Game does not predict loan repayment or savings.

4. **General Social Survey (GSS) trust/fairness/helpfulness questions also predict some financial outcomes**, especially repayment, but they do not predict savings as clearly.

Overall, the paper endorses the Trust Game as a field tool for measuring **trustworthiness**, but not as a pure measure of **trust**.

---

## 3. Background and Literature Context

### 3.1 Social capital and market failure
The paper begins from a standard development economics problem: market failures arise when contracts are difficult to enforce or monitor. Social capital can help solve these failures because trust and trustworthiness make informal contracting more feasible.

### 3.2 Individual-level social capital
Rather than treating social capital only as a group-level phenomenon, Karlan focuses on **individual-level social capital**, defined as the social skills and networks that allow individuals to overcome information and enforcement problems.

Important concepts include:
- **Trust**
- **Trustworthiness**
- Ability to sustain informal contracts
- Social networks and social sanctions

### 3.3 Link to prior trust literature
Previous studies had shown:
- Trust Game behavior correlates with attitudes and relationships.
- GSS trust/fairness/helpfulness responses correlate with broad social and economic outcomes.
- But prior work had not directly linked Trust Game behavior to **actual future economic decisions** such as loan repayment.

This paper's innovation is to "complete the circle" by linking:
- survey measures,
- experimental game behavior,
- and real-world financial outcomes.

---

## 4. Study Setting: FINCA in Peru

### 4.1 Institutional context
The field site is **FINCA**, a nonprofit village banking organization in **Ayacucho, Peru**.

FINCA provides:
- four-month loans,
- to groups of about 30 poor women,
- primarily to support small businesses.

### 4.2 Group lending structure
The lending model is group-based:
- each woman has her own loan,
- but all group members are ultimately responsible for repayment,
- nobody gets a new loan until all previous loans are repaid.

This creates a setting where social pressure, monitoring, and trust matter heavily.

### 4.3 Savings component
Participants also save through the program:
- part of savings is mandatory,
- additional voluntary savings are possible,
- savings effectively remain at risk because group default can affect them.

This makes **saving** another revealing behavioral outcome:
- if savings are exposed to peers' repayment behavior,
- then choosing to save can be interpreted as an act involving trust in others.

### 4.4 Why this setting matters
This microfinance environment is ideal for the paper because:
- financial contracts are only partly formal,
- repayment depends significantly on social enforcement,
- trustworthiness should matter for actual outcomes,
- and trust-related measures can be tested in a meaningful field setting.

---

## 5. Data Sources and Sample

The study draws on three main sources of data:

1. **Private individual survey**
   - demographics,
   - GSS-style trust questions,
   - religion and related measures.

2. **Public group survey**
   - existing relationships among group members,
   - social ties,
   - familiarity,
   - cultural similarity,
   - geographic closeness.

3. **Administrative financial data**
   - default outcomes,
   - dropout due to default or discipline,
   - voluntary savings,
   - borrowing behavior one year after the games.

### Sample
The games were conducted with **864 FINCA members**.  
The trust-game subsamples differ slightly depending on whether a person was observed as Player A or Player B and whether all supporting variables were available.

---

## 6. Experimental Design

## 6.1 The Trust Game

The Trust Game is the paper's main experimental tool.

### Rules
- Both players begin with **3 coins**.
- Individuals are randomly assigned to be **Player A** or **Player B**.
- Player A can send **0, 1, 2, or 3 coins** to Player B.
- The administrator **matches** the amount sent.
- Player B then chooses how much of the received amount to return to Player A.

### Interpretation
Traditionally:
- **Player A's sending** = trust
- **Player B's returning** = trustworthiness or reciprocity

### Equilibrium benchmark
Under standard game-theoretic logic with no future consequences:
- Player B should return nothing,
- therefore Player A should send nothing.

But in practice, many players do send and return positive amounts.

### Practical implementation details
Because many participants had limited literacy:
- rules were explained orally,
- in **Spanish and Quechua**,
- participants could observe their partner's identity,
- but were separated and not allowed to communicate.

This design aimed to preserve understanding while limiting direct collusion or negotiation.

---

## 6.2 Trust Game Results: Descriptive Patterns

### Player A decisions
Out of 397 Player A observations:
- 23% sent 0 coins,
- 39% sent 1 coin,
- 17% sent 2 coins,
- 22% sent 3 coins.

Thus, most players sent something, despite the subgame-perfect prediction of zero.

### Player B decisions
Player B's returns varied, but positive reciprocity was common.

Key pattern:
- the more A sent, the more room B had to return,
- yet average returns still made sending a poor financial bet for A.

### Expected return to Player A
On average:
- sending 1 coin yielded about **0.89** back,
- sending 2 yielded about **1.71** back,
- sending 3 yielded about **2.53** back.

This means Player A generally received **less than was sent**, supporting Karlan's argument that high sending may not reflect savvy trust, but possibly poor investment judgment or risk-seeking behavior.

---

## 6.3 The Public Goods Game

Karlan also ran a Public Goods Game to compare whether a different cooperative measure predicts real behavior.

### Rules
- Conducted in 41 lending groups.
- Each participant received 1 coin.
- Each privately chose whether to contribute it.
- If at least **80%** of the group contributed, everyone received 2 coins.

### Purpose
This game was intended to capture cooperative or group-oriented behavior.

### Descriptive result
Contribution rates ranged from **55.6% to 100%**, averaging **80.7%**.

### Why include this game?
The Public Goods Game offers a comparison:
- if general cooperation predicts repayment, it should perform similarly to the Trust Game;
- if not, then the Trust Game may be measuring something more specific and behaviorally relevant.

---

## 7. Key Variables and Measures

### 7.1 Financial outcomes
The paper studies three main future financial outcomes:

1. **Default**
   - amount unpaid on the loan after one year.

2. **Dropped out due to default or discipline**
   - a narrower, arguably less noisy indicator of problematic repayment behavior.

3. **Total voluntary savings contributions**
   - extra savings beyond required amounts.

### 7.2 Social connectedness measures
The paper constructs multiple measures of social ties:
- proportion of group sharing similar culture,
- distance to other group members,
- proportion living within a 10-minute walk,
- side borrowing from other members,
- ability to name members from memory,
- church attendance overlap,
- whether partners attended the same small church,
- whether the partner was known personally,
- party attendance and invitations.

### 7.3 Cultural identity
Participants were classified into cultural categories:
- indigenous,
- mixed,
- Western,

based on observable markers such as language, clothing, and appearance.

These variables are used to study whether trust and reciprocity depend on social similarity.

---

## 8. Determinants of Trust Game Behavior

## 8.1 What predicts Player A sending?

Player A behavior is influenced by several relational and demographic variables.

### Stronger sending by Player A is associated with:
- **both players being indigenous**,
- **geographic proximity** (partner living within a 10-minute walk),
- **attending the same small church**,
- **higher education**,
- **older age**.

These findings support the idea that social closeness and shared identity can raise what looks like trusting behavior.

### Lower sending by Player A is associated with:
- **being financially cautious** (saving more than borrowing),
- certain church-attendance patterns,
- having more side-borrowing relationships.

Karlan emphasizes the especially important result that people who **do not maximize available debt** - interpreted as more financially cautious - send **less** as Player A. This is one of the main reasons he doubts that Player A behavior is pure trust.

---

## 8.2 Karlan's reinterpretation of Player A: trust or gamble?

One of the most important arguments in the paper is that Player A's behavior is confounded.

### Why?
Because passing money is, on average, a **bad investment**. If Player A sends more despite poor expected returns, this may indicate:
- risk tolerance,
- gambling propensity,
- poor investment judgment,
- willingness to "play,"
not simply trust.

Karlan reinforces this with anecdotal field observations: some participants reportedly said things equivalent to **"I am going to play"** when choosing whether to pass money.

### Implication
This means the standard interpretation:
- "more sent = more trust"

is too simplistic. Player A behavior appears to mix:
- trust,
- social expectations,
- and risk-taking.

This is a central conceptual contribution of the chapter.

---

## 8.3 What predicts Player B returning?

Player B behavior is interpreted as trustworthiness or reciprocity, and the patterns are more convincing.

### More returning by Player B is associated with:
- **positive GSS trust/fairness/helpfulness responses**,
- **more side-contract borrowing from peers**,
- some evidence of **geographic embeddedness**,
- and possibly partner/group characteristics that increase accountability.

### Geographic interpretation
Player B returns less when she lives farther from the group overall. Karlan notes that this may reflect:
- lower trustworthiness,
- or less fear of social sanctions / reprisal.

This raises an important distinction:
- **innate trustworthiness**
vs.
- **trustworthy behavior induced by social pressure**.

The paper argues that both may matter, but later evidence suggests Player B behavior still captures something stable and individually meaningful.

---

## 8.4 Role of culture and proximity

The paper finds several nuanced relationship effects:

- When **both partners are indigenous**, Player A sends substantially more.
- If **Player A is indigenous and Player B is Western**, Player B returns more.
- If the partner lives nearby, Player A sends more.
- If partners share a smaller church, Player A sends much more.

These results suggest that trust-related behavior depends on:
- cultural affinity,
- social familiarity,
- local interaction networks,
- expected future contact.

---

## 9. Public Goods Game Determinants

The Public Goods Game reveals a somewhat different behavioral profile.

### Individuals are more likely to contribute when:
- they pass more in the Trust Game,
- they borrow more from peers through side-contracts.

### Individuals are less likely to contribute when:
- they live farther from the group,
- they have not attended church recently,
- they are coded as Western.

At the group level, groups with stronger GSS prosocial responses contribute more on average.

### Interpretation
This game seems to measure some form of generalized cooperative participation, but the paper later shows that it does **not** predict the financial outcomes of interest.

---

## 10. Predicting Financial Decisions

This is the empirical heart of the paper.

Karlan tests whether behavior in the games predicts future financial outcomes after controlling for many observable characteristics.

## 10.1 Player B behavior predicts repayment and savings

This is the strongest and clearest result.

People who return more as **Player B**:
- have **lower loan default**,
- are **less likely to drop out due to default or discipline**,
- and tend to have **higher voluntary savings**.

### Why this matters
These patterns strongly support the interpretation of Player B behavior as **trustworthiness**.

The finding is especially important because it connects a lab-style measure directly to a real-world financial contract that depends on social enforcement.

---

## 10.2 Player A behavior predicts worse financial outcomes

The results for Player A go in the opposite direction from what a pure-trust story would suggest.

People who pass more as **Player A**:
- are **more likely to drop out** due to default or discipline,
- have **lower voluntary savings**,
- and do not show better repayment outcomes.

### Why this matters
If Player A behavior were really just trust, one might expect:
- more savings,
- stronger participation,
- and better contract performance.

Instead, the negative relationship supports Karlan's interpretation that sending may reflect:
- bad risk-taking,
- gambling,
- or poor judgment under uncertainty.

This is the paper's most provocative result.

---

## 10.3 Public Goods Game does not predict financial outcomes

The Public Goods Game has essentially **no predictive power** for:
- default,
- dropout,
- or savings.

### Interpretation
Cooperation in a generic collective-action setting is not the same thing as the kind of trustworthiness needed for loan repayment in group lending.

This result strengthens the paper's claim that the Trust Game - specifically Player B's behavior - captures something more relevant to microfinance contract performance.

---

## 10.4 GSS trust/fairness/helpfulness questions

The paper also evaluates survey-based social capital measures.

### Findings
More positive answers to the GSS questions, especially relative to society as a whole:
- predict **less default**,
- predict **lower dropout due to default or discipline**,
- but do **not** clearly predict savings.

### Interpretation
These survey questions appear to track **trustworthy tendencies** better than trusting behavior, consistent with prior research and with this paper's findings about Player B.

---

## 10.5 Same-group versus different-group partner effects

Karlan tests whether predictive power comes only from playing with someone in one's own lending group.

### Result
The predictive relationship remains even when accounting for whether the game partner came from the same group.

### Significance
This suggests the Trust Game is not merely detecting:
- immediate group dynamics,
- partner-specific future interaction,
- or fear of retaliation from that exact partner.

Instead, Player B behavior appears to reveal a more general underlying trait related to trustworthiness.

---

## 11. Conceptual Contributions

## 11.1 Trust and trustworthiness should be separated
The paper insists that **trust** and **trustworthiness** are analytically distinct and should not be collapsed into a single social-capital measure.

## 11.2 Player A is a contaminated measure
Player A's action mixes:
- trust,
- social familiarity,
- expectations of sanction,
- and risk-taking.

This makes it unreliable as a pure trust indicator.

## 11.3 Player B is a more credible behavioral measure
Player B's action is more stable and more directly linked to real repayment behavior, so it is a better measure of trustworthiness.

## 11.4 Experimental economics can matter in the field
The paper shows that experimental games can predict meaningful real-world outcomes when used carefully in relevant institutional settings.

---

## 12. Practical Implications

### For microfinance
The findings suggest that:
- trustworthiness among borrowers matters for repayment success,
- identifying trustworthy individuals could improve group lending outcomes,
- and social capital is not just an abstract concept; it has measurable financial consequences.

### For development economics
The paper supports the use of field experiments to study:
- contract enforcement,
- informal finance,
- social norms,
- and the role of trust in poor communities.

### For measurement
Researchers should be cautious when labeling game behaviors:
- not everything called "trust" is actually trust,
- behavioral measures need validation against real-world outcomes.

---

## 13. Limits and Nuances

The paper is careful not to overclaim.

### Important caveats
- Player B behavior may still reflect some **fear of reprisal**, not only pure internal trustworthiness.
- Social proximity matters, so context still influences game behavior.
- The setting is specific: poor women in a Peruvian group-lending program.
- The paper does not show whether trustworthiness can be **created**, only that it can be **measured** and that it matters.

These caveats do not erase the main findings, but they sharpen their interpretation.

---

## 14. Final Conclusion

Karlan concludes that the Trust Game is useful - but only with qualification.

### Final takeaways
- **Player B returns** are a valid and practically meaningful measure of **trustworthiness**.
- **Player A sending** should not be treated as a clean measure of trust because it is confounded with **risk-taking/gambling**.
- The Trust Game can predict real-world financial behavior in a setting where social enforcement matters.
- The Public Goods Game does not offer the same predictive value here.
- Trustworthiness is a key ingredient in the success of group lending programs.

In short, the paper reframes the Trust Game from a broad measure of "social capital" into a more precise instrument: one that is especially informative about **trustworthiness**, and therefore about who is likely to honor informal financial obligations.

## 15. Quick Study Guide

### Core research question
Can experimental measures of trust and trustworthiness predict real financial behavior in microfinance?

### Most important result
**Player B behavior predicts repayment; Player A behavior predicts worse savings/default-related outcomes.**

### Most important conceptual contribution
The paper challenges the assumption that **sending in the Trust Game = trust**.

### Most exam-worthy contrast
- **Trust Game (Player B)** -> predictive of repayment
- **Public Goods Game** -> not predictive
- **GSS questions** -> predict repayment better than savings

### Most important interpretation
Trustworthiness appears to be a real and measurable individual trait with consequences for credit markets, while "trust" as measured by Player A is contaminated by risk preference.
